Vaclav Havel was a Czech statesman, writer and former dissident, who served as the last President of Czechoslovakia from 1989 until the dissolution of Czechoslovakia in 1992 and then as the first President of the Czech Republic from 1993 to 2003. As a writer of Czech literature, he is known for his plays, essays, and memoirs.

His educational opportunities having been limited by his bourgeois background, Havel first rose to prominence as a playwright. In works such as The Garden Party and The Memorandum, Havel used an absurdist style to criticize communism. After participating in the Prague Spring and being blacklisted after the invasion of Czechoslovakia, he became more politically active and helped found several dissident initiatives, including Charter 77 and the Committee for the Defense of the Unjustly Prosecuted. His political activities brought him under the surveillance of the secret police and he spent multiple stints in prison, the longest being nearly four years, between 1979 and 1983.

Havel's Civic Forum party played a major role in the Velvet Revolution that toppled communism in Czechoslovakia in 1989. He assumed the presidency shortly thereafter, and was re-elected in a landslide the following year and after Slovak independence in 1993. Havel was instrumental in dismantling the Warsaw Pact and expanding NATO membership eastward.

Havel's political philosophy was one of anti-consumerism, humanitarianism, environmentalism, civil activism, and direct democracy. He supported the Czech Green Party from 2004 until his death. He received numerous rewards during his lifetime including the Presidential Medal of Freedom, the Gandhi Peace Prize, the Philadelphia Liberty Medal, the Order of Canada, the Four Freedoms Award, the Ambassador of Conscience Award, and the Hanno R. Ellenbogen Citizenship Award.

Main dates in biography of Vaclav Havel:

  • 5 October 1936: born into a wealthy family of a real estate developer.
  • Early 1950s: because of his class background, Havel entered into a four-year apprenticeship as a chemical laboratory assistant and simultaneously took evening classes at a gymnasium.
  • 1954: completed his secondary education.
  • 1959: started employment in Prague's theatre world as a stagehand at Prague's Theatre ABC after completing military srvice.
  • Same 1959: became a student of dramatic arts by correspondence at the Theatre Faculty of the Academy of Performing Arts in Prague.
  • 1963: Havel's full-length play 'The Garden Party' performed in public and won him international acclaim.
  • 1968: Havel's play 'The Memorandum', one of his best known plays, was brought to The Public Theater in New York.
  • Same 1968: following the suppression of the Prague Spring in 1968, Havel was banned from the theatre and became more politically active..
  • 1977: earned reputation of a leading dissident with the publication of the Charter 77 manifesto.
  • 1979: Havel co-founded the Committee for the Defense of the Unjustly Prosecuted.
  • May 1979: his longest stay in prison started.
  • 1982: short play by Samuel Beckett, Catastrophe, was dedicated to Havel while he was held as a political prisoner
  • 29 December 1989: while he was leader of the Civic Forum, Havel became President of Czechoslovakia by a unanimous vote of the Federal Assembly.
  • 26 January 1993: won Presindential elections, when the Czech Republic was created as one of two successor states.
  • January 1996: Olga Havlová, his wife of 32 years, died of cancer at 62.
  • December 1996: Havel was diagnosed with lung cancer.
  • 1997: he remarried, to actress Dagmar Veškrnová.
  • 1998 Havel was re-ellected President.
  • 2003 he left the office after his second presidential term had ended.
  • 2007 Havel's first new play in almost two decades, Leaving, was published.
  • 2008 became a Member of the European Council on Tolerance and Reconciliation.
  • 18 December 2011: died at age 75, at his country home in Hrádeček.
